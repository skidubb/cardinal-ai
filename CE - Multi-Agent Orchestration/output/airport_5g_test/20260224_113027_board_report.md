**Universal Synthesis:** Every constituency demands ≥99.95% uptime with financial consequences for failure. **Binding Principle:** *Uptime SLAs are zone-specific and use-case-specific, measured at the point of consumption (edge device, POS terminal, AGV controller), not at the core. Penalties scale to the operational impact of the constituency served.*

# SECTION 3: KEY TENSIONS REQUIRING RESOLUTION

These are cross-constituency conflicts that cannot be resolved by any single pair. They require system-level architectural or governance decisions.

## CROSS-TENSION 1: Latency Heterogeneity Across the Campus

**Conflict:** Four different latency regimes are required simultaneously on the same physical campus.

| Zone | Requirement | Source | Stringency |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cargo warehouses | Sub-20ms local (AGV) | Cargo Director | Most stringent; safety-critical |
| Concessions POS | ≤30ms transaction processing | F&B Consortium | PCI-DSS compliance-adjacent |
| Airline ramp | Sub-50ms P95 under peak | Airline VP | High device density; coordination-critical |
| Cold chain telemetry | Sub-100ms end-to-end | Cargo Director | FDA regulatory floor |
| Digital signage / advertising | Sub-200ms | CRO | Least stringent; latency-tolerant |

**Why This Is Hard:** A single, campus-wide network architecture optimized for one latency profile will over-serve some zones and under-serve others. Optimizing for sub-20ms everywhere is prohibitively expensive. Optimizing for sub-200ms everywhere fails cargo and POS requirements.

**Resolution Direction:** Zone-specific RF engineering with differentiated edge compute placement. Cargo warehouses get dedicated distributed antenna systems with co-located edge compute. Terminal zones get edge compute for POS local breakout. Ramp zones are engineered for medium-density, medium-latency. This is a **zone-optimized architecture**, not a uniform deployment.

**Architectural Consequence:** The private 5G deployment is not one network. It is a family of zone-specific deployments sharing a common core, common governance, and common vendor ecosystem, but with differentiated RF design, edge compute placement, and SLA targets per zone.

## CROSS-TENSION 3: Spectrum Allocation Across Competing Density Requirements

**Conflict:** CBRS spectrum is finite. Six constituencies compete for capacity on shared spectrum.

| Constituency | Peak Device Count | Peak Timing | Spectrum Sensitivity |
|---|---|---|---|
| Cargo | 5,000–50,000 | 24/7; seasonal peaks | Highest density; industrial IoT |
| Airline | 300–500 iPads | Bank turns (predictable 2-hour windows) | Moderate density; time-concentrated |
| F&B/Concessions | ~1,000+ POS/signage/IoT | Passenger peak hours | Moderate; payment-critical |
| Passengers (offload) | 10,000+ concurrent | Peak travel days | Highest count; best-effort acceptable |
| CRO advertising | Moderate (signage endpoints) | Continuous | Latency-tolerant; low priority |
| DFW operations | Moderate (staff devices, IoT) | 24/7 | Mission-critical but low density |

**Why This Is Hard:** CBRS provides 150 MHz of spectrum (3550–3700 MHz) in tiered access. DFW's military proximity means Dynamic Protection Area (DPA) events can temporarily reduce available spectrum. During a DPA event coinciding with a cargo peak sort window and an airline bank turn, spectrum scarcity is real.

**Resolution Direction:** Spectrum allocation by function, not constituency (from Pair 3 resolution):
- **Slice 1 (Low-latency operational control):** Airline ramp + Cargo AGV + Cold chain. Dedicated n77 channels. Time-division multiplexed during natural load-balancing windows.
- **Slice 2 (Transaction processing):** POS, payment, operational telemetry. Guaranteed minimum QoS. CBRS priority access.
- **Slice 3 (Bulk data & passenger offload):** Best-effort. CBRS general access + opportunistic n78.
- **Slice 4 (Failover/backup):** Existing macro cellular.

Combined with DPA event handling protocols validated before go-live (from Pair 1 shared requirement).

**Architectural Consequence:** The slicing architecture must be function-based, not constituency-based. This means airline and cargo share a low-latency slice (with sub-allocation by time and zone), POS and operational telemetry share a transaction slice, and passenger/advertising traffic gets best-effort capacity. This is more spectrally efficient than giving each constituency its own dedicated allocation.

## CROSS-TENSION 4: Centralized vs. Distributed Control Authority

**Conflict:** CIO wants unified visibility. AT&T wants operational autonomy. Airline wants NOC control of its slice. Cargo operators each want independent tenant domains. CRO wants to manage revenue platform. F&B wants zero management burden.

| Constituency | Control Preference | Scope |
|---|---|---|
| CIO | Unified visibility + policy authority | Entire campus |
| AT&T | Autonomous carrier layer operations | RAN, SAS, field support |
| Airline | Root-level control of ramp operations slice | Terminal-specific |
| Cargo (FedEx) | Independent tenant configuration domain | Warehouse-specific |
| Cargo (UPS) | Independent tenant configuration domain | Warehouse-specific |
| CRO | Revenue platform management | Commercial analytics layer |
| F&B | Zero management responsibility | Per-location connectivity |

**Why This Is Hard:** Seven distinct control preferences cannot all be "in charge." Centralization (CIO controls everything) alienates AT&T, airline, and cargo. Full distribution (everyone controls their piece) creates coordination chaos during cross-cutting events (DPA spectrum reduction, campus-wide security incident, major weather event).

**Resolution Direction:** Three-tier control architecture:
1. **Infrastructure layer (DFW-controlled):** Physical infrastructure, core network, backhaul, RF engineering, spectrum coordination. DFW has final authority. Changes require 30-day stakeholder notice.
2. **Slice layer (Constituency-controlled):** Each constituency controls configuration within their allocated slice. Airline NOC controls ramp slice. Cargo operators control their tenant domains. CRO controls commercial analytics layer. Changes within a slice require no cross-constituency approval.
3. **Carrier operations layer (AT&T-controlled):** RAN operations, SAS coordination, field support, emergency response. AT&T acts autonomously within contracted SLAs. DFW observes via APIs and retains veto authority over decisions affecting broader campus.

**Escalation for cross-cutting conflicts:** Standing architecture board (monthly); executive steering committee (quarterly); emergency protocol for real-time conflicts (DFW operations center has final authority during campus-wide events).

| F&B | Stability; 90-day parallel operation; real POS validation | Low (POS outages = $7M+ per weekend) |
| Airline | 6-month burn-in before any commitment | Very low (operational safety) |
| Cargo | 18-month infrastructure embedding, then software deployment | Low (construction immovable) |
| CIO | Phased with rollback; no irreversible decisions | Low (87M passengers at stake) |
| AT&T | Operational proof before scaling | Moderate (reputation risk) |

**Resolution Direction:** Revenue starts small and early (Month 12 pilot subscriptions, early advertising in controlled environment) while operational validation proceeds in parallel. The CRO's $80M target is achievable on a phased timeline: Y1 $500K → Y2 $3–5M → Y3–5 $80M+ cumulative. This satisfies the CRO's need for early board-facing proof points without forcing premature full-campus deployment.

**Key Insight:** The CRO's competitive window concern is valid, but the actual competitive advantage is *having the infrastructure and proving it works*, not *monetizing at scale before it's reliable*. LAX and ATL aren't deploying private 5G yet. DFW's advantage is being first *and* being right. A failed deployment that takes down holiday POS systems is far more damaging to competitive position than a 6-month delay in advertising revenue.

## CROSS-TENSION 6: Data Monetization vs. Privacy/Trust Across All Parties

**Conflict:** CRO wants to create a data products business. F&B operators fear surveillance-driven rent increases. Airlines consider their operational data proprietary. Cargo operators have competitive intelligence concerns (FedEx data visible to UPS). CIO needs security visibility into all traffic.

| Data Type | Who Generates It | Who Wants It | Who Fears Exposure |
|---|---|---|---|
| Passenger flow patterns | DFW infrastructure | CRO (monetization) | Privacy regulators |
| Operator transaction data | F&B tenants | CRO (analytics products) | F&B operators (rent leverage) |
| Airline operational telemetry | Airline devices | Airline NOC (exclusive) | Airline (competitive intelligence) |
| Cargo logistics data | Cargo operators | Cargo ops (exclusive) | Cargo (competitive intelligence) |
| Network security events | All traffic | CIO (SIEM) | All parties (surveillance concern) |

**Resolution Direction:** The three-tier data governance model from Pair 2 must extend campus-wide:
- **Tier 1 (Airport-generated, DFW-owned):** Aggregated, anonymized flow patterns. No constituency-specific data. Monetizable by DFW.
- **Tier 2 (Constituency-controlled):** Each party owns data generated by their devices and operations. DFW can offer analytics services but access is opt-in with revenue sharing.
- **Tier 3 (Privacy/security-protected):** Individual-level data encrypted end-to-end. DFW security monitoring is limited to metadata and threat signatures, not content inspection.

**Contractual Binding:** No constituency's Tier 2 data can be shared with another constituency without explicit written consent. Airline data never visible to cargo operators. Cargo operator A data never visible to cargo operator B. F&B operator sales data never used for rent adjustment without consent. This must be architecturally enforced (encryption, access controls), not just policy-enforced.

**Strengths:**
- **Best latency performance.** Edge compute co-located with each zone means sub-20ms cargo AGV, sub-30ms POS, and sub-50ms ramp operations are all architecturally achievable without backhaul dependency. Directly addresses Cross-Tension 1.
- **Maximum isolation.** Each cargo operator gets a physically separate edge node. FedEx data never traverses UPS infrastructure. Airline ramp data stays in ramp edge. This is the strongest possible answer to Cross-Tension 6 (data privacy/trust).
- **Autonomous zone operation.** If backhaul between zones fails, each zone continues operating independently. This provides the highest resilience — directly addresses the 99.99% universal requirement.
- **Natural fit for construction integration.** Each edge node is a defined physical deployment embedded in a specific construction project. Cargo warehouses get their edge node embedded during construction. Terminal F gets its edge node during fit-out. No cross-zone dependencies.
- **Parallel deployment tracks are inherent.** Each edge node is an independent deployment. Cargo deploys on cargo timeline. Terminal F deploys on Terminal F timeline. Cross-Tension 2 (timeline convergence) is resolved by architecture.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Highest capital cost.** Multiple edge compute nodes, multiple local UPF/AMF instances, redundant backhaul to each — this is the most expensive architecture. May conflict with F-10 (construction-embedded capex) and F-3 (tenant cost ceiling).
- **Operational complexity is extreme.** AT&T must manage 5–8+ independent edge nodes, each with different configurations. MTTR increases because field engineers need zone-specific expertise. Conflicts with AT&T's preference for platform uniformity.
- **Cross-zone analytics are hardest here.** CRO's Tier 1 data (aggregated passenger flow across terminals) requires collecting data from multiple independent edge nodes and correlating it. This adds latency and complexity to the analytics pipeline.
- **Spectrum coordination across distributed nodes is harder.** SAS must coordinate spectrum allocation for each node independently. DPA events must trigger coordinated response across all nodes simultaneously. This is technically solvable but adds system complexity.
- **Vendor management multiplied.** If each edge node uses the same vendor, it's manageable but creates the same lock-in concern. If different vendors per zone, interoperability and governance complexity is extreme.

**Constituencies Best Served:** Cargo Director (isolation, latency, autonomous operation), Airline VP (zone control, resilience), F&B Consortium (POS local breakout, isolation)

**Constituencies Underserved:** AT&T (operational complexity), CRO (cross-zone analytics), CIO (management complexity, cost)

**Key Risk:** Over-engineering. The distributed architecture may deliver capabilities beyond what most zones need, at a cost that undermines the financial model. Sub-20ms latency is critical for cargo AGVs but unnecessary for digital signage — yet this architecture builds edge compute everywhere.


**Strengths:**
- **Lowest initial risk and cost.** Phase 1 is simple, cheap, and reversible. Directly satisfies the universal rollback requirement.
- **Data-driven architecture decisions.** Instead of designing the full campus architecture based on vendor promises and theoretical RF models, each phase's architecture is informed by actual DFW operational data. This is the strongest answer to Cross-Tension 2 (timeline convergence) because it doesn't try to make all decisions simultaneously.
- **AT&T's preferred model.** Single vendor, AT&T-operated, growing complexity over time. AT&T can build operational maturity incrementally. Addresses AT&T's concern about immature open RAN platforms.
- **CRO's phased monetization fits naturally.** Revenue starts small in Phase 1 (pilot subscriptions), grows in Phase 2 (advertising, analytics), reaches full scale in Phase 3–4. Aligns revenue ambition with operational maturity.
- **Vendor diversification is earned, not forced.** Multi-vendor policy kicks in at Phase 3 only if scale justifies it. CIO gets the contractual commitment now but execution happens when the architecture can absorb the complexity.
- **Construction integration works because decisions are made incrementally.** Terminal F architecture locks based on Phase 1 data (12+ months). Cargo infrastructure embeds based on platform selection (Q2 2025). Terminal C migrates based on Phase 2 validation. No one makes irreversible decisions based on insufficient data.

**Weaknesses:**
- **Cargo timeline conflict is the critical flaw.** Cargo needs spec lock by Q3 2025 and operational readiness by Q1 2027. This architecture has Phase 1 still in burn-in when cargo specs must lock. Cargo cannot wait for Phase 1 data to inform their infrastructure decisions. **This is the same tension identified in Pair 3 (Tension B) and requires the parallel-track resolution regardless of which hypothesis is chosen.**
- **Sub-20ms cargo latency may not be achievable in Phase 1's minimal architecture.** If cargo deploys in Phase 2 using Phase 1's architecture (centralized core, no local edge), cargo AGV latency requirements may not be met. Edge compute for cargo may need to be a Phase 2 design requirement determined *before* Phase 1 data is available.
- **CRO may view this as too conservative.** "Start simple" can become "stay simple" if phase gates create bureaucratic inertia. The CRO needs contractual assurance that phase advancement has deadlines, not just quality gates.
- **Airline's Terminal C migration is pushed to Phase 3 (24–48 months out).** Airline VP may find this acceptable (they want extensive validation), but the 80% of traffic in existing terminals remains on legacy infrastructure for 3+ years.

**Constituencies Best Served:** CIO (phased risk, data-driven), AT&T (operational simplicity, progressive complexity), CRO (phased monetization alignment)

**Constituencies Underserved:** Cargo Director (timeline mismatch), Airline VP (Terminal C delay), F&B Consortium (pilot timeline may feel slow)

**Key Risk:** Phase 1 results are ambiguous (not clearly good or bad), creating decision paralysis at the Phase 2 gate. The architecture must include clear, quantitative go/no-go criteria for each phase transition to prevent indefinite "evaluation."

### Winning Architecture: Hybrid Co-Investment (H3)
**Confidence: High**

The Hybrid Co-Investment model — where DFW owns the physical and logical infrastructure while AT&T operates the carrier-grade radio and spectrum layer with revenue sharing on multi-tenant access — is the only architecture that produces zero inconsistencies across all five diagnostic evidence streams and all six constituency groups.

This architecture resolves the fundamental tension at the heart of DFW's private 5G decision: the airport needs sovereign control over data, security posture, and long-term vendor destiny (demanded by IT leadership, federal regulators, and airline tenants), but it simultaneously cannot afford to build carrier-grade spectrum management competency from scratch or jeopardize the $10M live CWP serving 87M passengers. H3 threads this needle by splitting the stack at the natural seam — infrastructure ownership below, carrier operations above — giving DFW the accountability layer that federal cybersecurity mandates require and the data ownership layer that unlocks $6-13M in annual monetization, while preserving AT&T's operational role where its competency is genuinely irreplaceable: real-time SAS coordination, DPA event handling near military installations, and CBRS spectrum optimization across simultaneous cargo, ramp, and passenger workloads.

The revenue-share mechanism on tenant access (airlines, cargo operators, concessionaires) aligns AT&T's incentives with network quality rather than subscription extraction, while DFW's infrastructure ownership structurally guarantees the vendor portability that all six constituencies independently demanded. H4 (Phased Sovereignty) was the nearest competitor with only two inconsistencies, but its bifurcated ownership model — AT&T-led in legacy terminals, airport-owned in new builds — creates a permanent dual-architecture governance problem that compounds operational complexity and fragments the security perimeter that TSA/DHS requires to be unified and auditable.

**Confidence Reasoning:** The quantitative separation is definitive: H3 has zero inconsistencies versus two for the next-best hypothesis (H4) and three each for H1 and H2. The two most diagnostic evidence items (E5-security and E5-data monetization, both at 0.75 diagnosticity) are both fully consistent with H3 and inconsistent with at least one competing hypothesis. The result passes a face-validity test: hybrid infrastructure-ownership-plus-carrier-operations models are the emerging pattern in airport, port, and smart-city private 5G deployments. The only factor preventing unconditional certainty is that the matrix does not capture execution risk — H3's contractual and governance complexity is real, and the evidence set does not include a direct measure of AT&T's willingness to accept the operator-not-owner role.

### Key Differentiators

- **Security (diagnosticity 0.75):** Federal cybersecurity mandates require DFW to be accountable for the security architecture, route all events to DFW's SIEM, and respond immediately to federal directives. Only an ownership model gives DFW the authority to enforce cryptographic tenant isolation, audit independently, and make unilateral emergency changes. H1 leaves DFW dependent on AT&T's security responsiveness; H2 achieves it but at the cost of spectrum management competency gaps.

- **Data Monetization (diagnosticity 0.75):** The $6-13M annual data monetization opportunity requires network-level data ownership, not application-layer scraping. H3's infrastructure ownership gives DFW structural control over the data pipeline — from raw packet flows to anonymized Tier 1 analytics products — without requiring DFW to also operate the radio layer.

- **Vendor Portability (diagnosticity 0.50):** All six constituencies independently rejected single-vendor dependency. H3 uniquely satisfies this by giving DFW infrastructure-layer ownership with contractual breakout provisions, enabling component-level vendor substitution at the 10-year lifecycle's Phase 3+ multi-vendor trigger.

### Residual Concerns

1. **AT&T's willingness to accept an operator-not-owner role.** AT&T's enterprise division may view this as a downgrade from their current CWP position. If AT&T refuses these terms, DFW faces a choice between compromising on ownership structure or seeking an alternative carrier operator, introducing transition risk to the live CWP.

2. **Governance complexity in the owner-operator split.** A DPA event during holiday peak volume requires sub-second spectrum reallocation — there is no time for governance escalation. The contract must pre-authorize AT&T's operational autonomy within defined bounds while preserving DFW's sovereign override for security directives.

3. **Capital expenditure burden.** Airport-owned infrastructure means DFW bears CapEx for small cells, edge compute, fiber backhaul, and MEC across all terminals, cargo facilities, and airfield zones. The business case depends on tenant adoption velocity and data monetization ramp.

4. **Spectrum management succession.** If AT&T's contract is terminated, DFW must have a warm-standby path to alternative spectrum management. DPA exposure near military installations means this is a hard operational requirement.

5. **Multi-tenant SLA enforcement.** The network must simultaneously serve safety-critical airline ramp ops (sub-10ms), TSA video analytics, cargo sort automation, passenger offload, and commercial IoT. SLA enforcement during congestion events requires sophisticated arbitration logic.
